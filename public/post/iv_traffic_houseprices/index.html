IV method with application to traffic and house prices

If we believe that a variable XXX causes (or affects) another variable YYY, we are often interested in studying how much the value of YYY changes if we make a small δx\delta xδx change in XXX. For example, how much do people dislike traffic on their street can be measured by decrease in house prices in response to increase in street traffic. The statistical technique most commonly used to answer causal questions is the linear regression [1]

Y=βX+Cγ+ϵ Y = \beta X + C\gamma + \epsilon Y=βX+Cγ+ϵ

where CCC is the matrix of control variables and we assume that standard OLS assumption holds. However, even when XXX and YYY are causally related, the estimate of β\betaβ is unbiased only if XXX and ϵ\epsilonϵ are independent conditioned on CCC. This is often not the case in practice. In our example, he construction of a new housing development in a neighborhood may increase traffic prices while lowering the house prices, but this decrease in prices would not be the effect of increased traffic. Similarly, if a shopping complex is built in a neighborhood, it may increase traffic and house prices without this being a causal effect. Instrumental Variable estimation addresses this endogeneity problem by using an instrument ZZZ that affects YYY only through XXX. By restricting to studying variation in YYY due to variation in XXX that originates through ZZZ, it is possible to obtain unbiased estimates of β\betaβ.

Let us build an intuititve understanding of omitted variable bias in OLS and how IV estimates remove the bias. Consider the causal relation descrbed in figure 1a. AAA is an omitted variable which confounds XXX and YYY. For concreteness, let’s assume that the true data generating process is given by

X=A+Z+ηY=2X+2A+ζ \begin{align*} X &= A+Z + \eta\\ Y &= 2X + 2A + \zeta \end{align*} XY​=A+Z+η=2X+2A+ζ​

Assume that AAA and ZZZ are inpendent, have mean 000 and variance 111. The length of line segments in Figure 1b represent the variation in the variables. Without accounting for the omitted variable, the standard OLS Y=βX+ϵ Y = \beta X + \epsilon Y=βX+ϵ gives the incorrect causal estimate

β^OLS=Cov(X,Y)Var(X)=3 \hat \beta_{OLS} = \frac{Cov(X,Y)}{Var(X)} = 3β^​OLS​=Var(X)Cov(X,Y)​=3

In the IV framework, we use an instrument ZZZ that affects YYY only through XXX (exogeneity requirement). The IV regression proceeds in 2 stages [2]

Stage 1:   X=δZ+η^  ⟹  X^=δ^IV1ZStage 2:   Y=βX^+ζ^=βδ^IV1Z+ζ^ \begin{align*} \text{Stage 1}&:~~~ X = \delta Z + \hat \eta \implies \hat X =\hat \delta_{IV1} Z \\ \text{Stage 2}&:~~~ Y = \beta \hat X + \hat \zeta = \beta \hat \delta_{IV1} Z + \hat \zeta \end{align*} Stage 1Stage 2​:   X=δZ+η^​⟹X^=δ^IV1​Z:   Y=βX^+ζ^​=βδ^IV1​Z+ζ^​​

In the stage 1, we isolate the variation in XXX caused by the variation in ZZZ given by X^=δ^IV1Z\hat X =\hat \delta_{IV1} ZX^=δ^IV1​Z. This is represented by the blue part of line segment representing XXX in Figure 1b. This variation is given independing of the omitted variable AAA, and we use this variation in X^\hat XX^ to identify the causal effect of XXX on YYY (represented by blue part of YYY’s line segment). The estimates are given by

δ^IV1=Cov(X,Z)Var(Z)=1β^IV2=Cov(X^,Y)Var(X^)=1δ^IV1Cov(Y,Z)Var(Z)=Cov(Y,Z)Cov(X,Z)=2 \begin{align*} \hat \delta_{IV1} &= \frac{Cov(X,Z)}{Var(Z)} = 1 \\ \hat \beta_{IV2} &= \frac{Cov(\hat X,Y)}{Var(\hat X)} = \frac{1}{\hat \delta_{IV1}} \frac{Cov(Y,Z)}{Var(Z)} = \frac{Cov(Y,Z)}{Cov(X,Z)} = 2 \end{align*} δ^IV1​β^​IV2​​=Var(Z)Cov(X,Z)​=1=Var(X^)Cov(X^,Y)​=δ^IV1​1​Var(Z)Cov(Y,Z)​=Cov(X,Z)Cov(Y,Z)​=2​

which is the correct causal effect of XXX on YYY. Although we only looked at the omitted variable bias, IV method is also useful to correct for “simultaneous causality bias” and the “Errors-in-variables bias”. To wrap our discussions, let us formally state the necessary conditions required for the IV method [3]

  1. Relevance: The instrument variable ZZZ should be correlated with the casual variable of interest XXX. This can be easily checked from significance level of the first stage estimate. Higher correlation in the first stage means that the instrument can more effectively extract the exogenous variation in the regressor and hence the causal estimate has a lower standard error.
  2. Exogeneity: The instrument variable is uncorrelated with the error term in the regression equation (Cor(Z,ϵ∣C)=0Cor(Z,\epsilon | C) = 0 Cor(Z,ϵ∣C)=0 where CCC is a vector of controls). Since the error term in unobserved, this condition is not statistically testable and needs to be justified theoretically. Sometimes, this is specified as the combination of exclusion restriction and as-if random condition.
    • Exclusion Restriction: Fixing controls CCC, the instrument ZZZ affects outcome YYY only through XXX.
    • As-if random: Fixing controls CCC, the instrument itself must not be endogenous. This rules out any reverse causality from YYY to XXX.
